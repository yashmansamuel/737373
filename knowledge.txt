Clinical Reasoning & Critical Thinking – 2026
----------------------------------------------

Sources:
- Real-world clinical practice principles
- Evidence-based medical reasoning

1. The 80/20 Rule in Diagnosis (Pareto Principle)
-------------------------------------------------
- 80% of diagnoses come from patient history alone.
- 10–15% require physical examination.
- Remaining 5% need expensive tests (CT, MRI, labs).
- Critical thinking prevents "over-testing" and emphasizes attentive listening.

2. Bayesian Reasoning (Probability-Based Thinking)
--------------------------------------------------
- Doctors think in probabilities, not just Yes/No.
- Pre-test Probability: Assess disease likelihood before testing.
- Positive test ≠ definite disease; consider false positives.
- Real-world application reduces unnecessary interventions.

3. Avoiding Cognitive Biases
----------------------------
- Anchoring Bias: Over-reliance on first information (e.g., chest burn → assume acidity, ignore heart attack).
- Premature Closure: Confirming diagnosis too early without considering differential diagnosis.
- Awareness of biases prevents medical errors.

4. “Zebra” vs. “Horse” (Occam’s Razor)
---------------------------------------
- Common symptoms often indicate common diseases (Flu, Infection), not rare conditions.
- Critical thinkers rule out common conditions first.
- Rare diagnoses are considered only when common causes are excluded.

5. Sunk Cost Fallacy in Treatment
---------------------------------
- Do not continue ineffective treatments just because resources are already spent.
- Critical thinkers pivot based on new patient data.

6. System 1 vs. System 2 Thinking
---------------------------------
- System 1 (Fast/Intuitive): Used in ER/emergencies (e.g., CPR initiation). Based on experience.
- System 2 (Slow/Analytical): Complex cases analyzed with detailed reports and tests.
- Effective clinicians switch between both systems as needed.

Summary – Clinical Thinking Process
----------------------------------
1. Observation: Patient behavior, speech, gait.
2. Hypothesis: List 3–4 possible diagnoses.
3. Deduction: Rule out possibilities via tests.
4. Verification: Assess improvement with treatment; adjust as needed.

Application:
- Can be applied to chest pain, headache, shortness of breath, and other real-world patient cases.
- Emphasizes reasoning over rote memorization.

1. The "Think Aloud" Protocol
-----------------------------
- Metacognition (thinking about thinking) is essential.
- Senior doctors verbalize their thought process to trainees:
    e.g., "I suspect cardiac failure because JVP is raised."
- Fact: Transparency in reasoning reduces medical errors by up to 30%.
- Term: Clinical Reasoning Transparency

2. Dual Process Theory (System 1 vs. System 2)
----------------------------------------------
- System 1 (Intuitive / Pattern Recognition):
    - Fast, recognizes diseases quickly (e.g., Parkinson’s via observed patterns)
    - Risk: Cognitive biases
- System 2 (Analytical / Slow Thinking):
    - Logical analysis of tests and symptoms for complex cases
- Real-world fact: 75% diagnostic errors occur when System 1 is over-relied upon and System 2 is underused

3. De-biasing Techniques (Checklists)
--------------------------------------
- Use checklists similar to aviation industry to avoid errors
- AI Integration: Human-in-the-loop reasoning
    - Doctors critically appraise AI-suggested diagnoses instead of blindly following
    - Ensures AI-assisted decisions are verified

4. Semantic Qualitative Drive (Data Synthesis)
-----------------------------------------------
- Symptoms are transformed into semantic qualifiers for better reasoning
    - Old: "Fever for 3 days"
    - New: "Acute, constant, high-grade febrile illness"
- Fact: Categorizing symptoms guides clinicians toward correct differential diagnosis automatically

5. Probability vs. Certainty (Bayesian Logic)
----------------------------------------------
- Medical decisions are based on probabilities, not 100% certainty
- Pre-test probability assessment prevents unnecessary tests
    - Example: 20-year-old with mild chest pain has low probability of heart attack → avoid Troponin test to reduce false positives

6. Premature Closure (The Number 1 Killer)
-------------------------------------------
- Danger: Accepting first diagnosis without considering alternatives
- Modern practice: "Diagnostic Time-outs"
    - Teams pause to ask: "What else could this be?"
    - Encourages exploration of differential diagnosis

Clinical Reasoning & Critical Thinking – 2025-26
------------------------------------------------

Sources:
- Real-world clinical practice observations
- Decision Science applied in medicine
- Cognitive psychology in clinical settings

1. The "Think Aloud" Protocol
-----------------------------
- Metacognition is essential in modern clinical teaching.
- Senior doctors verbalize their thought process to trainees.
- Fact: Transparency in reasoning reduces medical errors by up to 30%.
- Term: Clinical Reasoning Transparency.

2. Dual Process Theory (System 1 vs. System 2)
-----------------------------------------------
- System 1 (Intuitive): Fast pattern recognition; high risk of bias.
- System 2 (Analytical): Slow, logical analysis of symptoms and tests.
- Fact: 75% of diagnostic errors occur when System 1 is over-relied on without System 2 activation.

3. De-biasing Techniques (Checklists)
--------------------------------------
- Critical thinkers use structured checklists to avoid cognitive errors.
- Fact: With AI support, doctors critically appraise AI suggestions—Human-in-the-loop reasoning.

4. Semantic Qualitative Drive (Data Synthesis)
-----------------------------------------------
- Transform raw patient symptoms into semantic qualifiers.
- Example: "Fever for 3 days" → "Acute, constant, high-grade febrile illness."
- Fact: Categorizing symptoms improves differential diagnosis accuracy.

5. Probability vs. Certainty (Bayesian Logic)
----------------------------------------------
- Medical decisions are probabilistic, not 100% certain.
- Fact: Pre-test probability guides testing decisions; avoids unnecessary false positives.

6. Premature Closure (The Number 1 Killer)
-------------------------------------------
- Avoid locking onto the first diagnosis too soon.
- Modern hospitals use "Diagnostic Time-outs" to reassess alternative possibilities.

AGI-Level Reasoning & General Excellence – 2026
-----------------------------------------------

Sources:
- Decision Science
- Cognitive Psychology
- Learning & Problem-Solving Research
- AI Alignment & Meta-Learning Principles

1. Core Cognitive Skills
------------------------
- Observation: Carefully notice details before jumping to conclusions.
- Pattern Recognition: Identify repeated structures or trends in data.
- Abstraction: Generalize from specifics to principles; avoid overfitting to one example.
- Critical Thinking: Question assumptions; evaluate evidence; avoid cognitive biases.

2. Decision Science Principles
------------------------------
- Probability over Certainty: Always assess likelihoods, not absolutes.
- Cost-Benefit Analysis: Consider resources, time, and consequences before choosing actions.
- Trade-Off Awareness: Every choice has pros and cons; balance short-term and long-term impact.
- Feedback Loops: Integrate past outcomes to improve future decisions.

3. Dual-Process Thinking
------------------------
- System 1 (Fast/Intuitive): Quick judgments based on experience; useful under time pressure.
- System 2 (Slow/Analytical): Deep, stepwise reasoning; used for complex, uncertain problems.
- Effective Reasoners switch between both based on context.

4. Cognitive Bias Mitigation
----------------------------
- Anchoring Bias: Avoid over-reliance on first information.
- Confirmation Bias: Seek evidence that challenges assumptions.
- Premature Closure: Keep multiple hypotheses open until sufficient evidence is gathered.
- Sunk Cost Fallacy: Do not persist with failing approaches just because effort is already spent.

5. Metacognition & Self-Reflection
----------------------------------
- Think about thinking: Evaluate why you made decisions and what influenced them.
- Continuous Learning: Update mental models with new data.
- Self-Assessment: Regularly measure accuracy, efficiency, and bias in reasoning.

6. Problem-Solving Strategies
------------------------------
- Decomposition: Break complex problems into smaller, manageable parts.
- Hypothesis Testing: Generate multiple potential solutions; test them iteratively.
- Iterative Refinement: Adjust approach based on feedback and results.
- Simulation: Mentally simulate consequences before acting in the real world.

7. Knowledge Structuring & Semantic Organization
-------------------------------------------------
- Categorization: Group related concepts to simplify retrieval.
- Semantic Qualifiers: Assign meaningful descriptors to information for faster reasoning.
- Hierarchical Mapping: Build layers of general-to-specific knowledge for clarity.
- Cross-Domain Links: Connect concepts across domains for creative solutions.

8. Continuous Improvement
-------------------------
- Benchmarking: Compare decisions against best practices or expert models.
- Experimentation: Safely test new approaches and learn from outcomes.
- Reflection Journals: Record reasoning paths to improve future performance.
- Adaptive Learning: Prioritize learning areas where performance gaps exist.
